Non-Alcoholic Absinthe Hydrosol Recipe

Zero-proof concentrate, dilute 1:4 to 1:6

A zero-proof absinthe-style concentrate built around hydrosols, controlled bitterness, green finishing infusion, and an optional anise-fennel gum arabic emulsion for a louche-like water bloom.

The goal is not to mimic ethanol. The goal is to preserve the ritual: green bitterness, anise-fennel identity, cold dilution, and a glass that opens cloudy and aromatic under ice-cold water.

Concept

Four controllable parts

Hydrosol aromatic base
Volatile top notes from anise, fennel, coriander, and angelica without relying on beverage alcohol as the solvent.
Top-note control
Short bitter infusion
Wormwood and optional gentian held in a separate stock so bitterness can be dosed by taste instead of over-extracting the whole batch.
Structure
Green finishing infusion
Hyssop, lemon balm, and mint added late to keep the profile cold, green, and alive instead of muddy.
Fresh lift
Optional louche emulsion
A gum arabic oil emulsion that recreates the service effect of an absinthe louche without ethanol.
Water bloom

The hydrosol route is gentler and more repeatable than trying to transplant an alcoholic absinthe process directly into water.

Build Setup

What you need

  • Scale: 0.1 g accuracy; 0.01 g preferred for oils.
  • Heated vessel: small pot with lid or a lab beaker on a hot plate.
  • Filtration: fine mesh strainer plus coffee filters.
  • Shear: immersion blender or compact high-shear blender.
  • Storage: clean glass bottles, sanitized for longer hold times.
  • pH check: strips or a meter if you are benching multiple versions.

Ingredients

1 liter concentrate

Hydrosol aromatic base

Volatile structure

  • Anise hydrosol: 250 ml
  • Fennel hydrosol: 200 ml
  • Coriander hydrosol: 100 ml
  • Angelica hydrosol: 50 ml

Water and body

Roundness and final volume

  • Filtered water: 250 ml, plus process water and final adjustment
  • Vegetable glycerin: 80 ml
  • Simple syrup 1:1: 50 to 120 ml

Acid and seasoning

Brightness and balance

  • Citric acid: 2 to 4 g
  • Salt: 0.5 g

Bitter infusion

Separate and dose by taste

  • Dried grand wormwood: 0.25 to 0.5 g
  • Gentian root: 0.25 to 0.75 g, optional
  • Angelica root: 1.0 g, optional if angelica hydrosol is absent

Green finish

Late infusion only

  • Lemon balm: 3 g
  • Hyssop: 2 g
  • Spearmint or mint: 0.5 to 1 g

Optional color

Use cautiously

  • Food-grade chlorophyll or spinach extract: as needed

Use food-grade hydrosols. Hydrosol means the distilled aromatic water itself, not essential oil floated into plain water.

Procedure

Concentrate workflow

A. Bitter infusion

Controlled extraction

  1. Combine 150 ml filtered water, wormwood, gentian, and angelica root in a covered vessel.
  2. Heat to 75 to 85 C for 5 to 8 minutes only. Do not boil hard.
  3. Strain through fine mesh, then coffee filter.
  4. Cool fully. This becomes the bitter stock.

B. Green finishing infusion

Late fresh herb phase

  1. Heat 200 ml filtered water to 55 to 65 C.
  2. Add lemon balm, hyssop, and mint.
  3. Steep covered for 8 to 10 minutes.
  4. Strain and chill immediately.

C. Build the hydrosol base

Main blend

  1. Combine all hydrosols, glycerin, and 50 ml simple syrup in a clean vessel.
  2. Add the chilled green finishing infusion.
  3. Add 25 percent of the bitter stock and taste diluted at 1:5.
  4. Continue dosing bitter stock until the finish is clearly absinthe-like but not harsh.

D. Final adjustment

Balance and filtration

  1. Add citric acid gradually. The goal is brightness, not sourness.
  2. Add salt and tune sweetness.
  3. Bring total volume to 1 liter with filtered water.
  4. Chill overnight, then filter again if sediment forms.

Optional Louche Emulsion

Anise-fennel gum arabic emulsion

Use this only if the hydrosol concentrate tastes right but does not cloud enough when diluted. The result is a stable beverage emulsion, not a true ethanol-anethole louche, but the service effect can land very close visually.

Balance

Emulsion method

High-shear workflow

  1. Add gum arabic slowly to water while blending.
  2. Hydrate at least 4 hours, ideally overnight.
  3. Blend in glycerin, citric acid, and salt.
  4. Combine the oil phase separately.
  5. With the blender running, drizzle oil into the gum solution.
  6. Blend hard for 2 to 5 minutes, then rest overnight.

Suggested dosing

Per 100 ml finished drink

  • 0.5 g: light aroma, faint opalescence
  • 1.0 g: medium aroma, visible cloud
  • 1.5 g: strong aroma, milky jade bloom

Service

Serving ritual

  1. Add 15 to 25 ml concentrate to an absinthe glass.
  2. Optional: place a sugar cube on an absinthe spoon over the glass.
  3. Slowly drip or pour 90 to 125 ml ice-cold water over the sugar and into the concentrate.
  4. Watch for the green-to-jade water bloom, then stir gently.
  5. Target dilution: 1:4 for intense, 1:5 standard, 1:6 for a lighter aperitif profile.

Shelf Notes

Handling and stability

  • Use food-grade hydrosols, oils, glycerin, gum arabic, acids, and colors only.
  • Dose essential or flavor oils by weight, never by drops.
  • Keep wormwood very low and isolate it in a separate bitter stock.
  • For production work, validate residual alcohol, microbial stability, pH, shelf life, and thujone analytically.
  • Keep the finished profile dry, aromatic, and precise; sweetness should support the ritual, not dominate it.

Iteration

Adjustment notes

  • Too licorice or candy-like: reduce anise; add coriander, angelica, lemon balm, or citrus hydrosol.
  • Too bitter or medicinal: reduce bitter stock; add salt, sweetness, and acid balance.
  • Too flat: increase citric acid slightly and make a tiny salt correction.
  • No visible louche: add gum arabic emulsion at 0.5 to 1.5 g per 100 ml drink.
  • Too cloudy before dilution: reduce emulsion in concentrate and dose it at service instead.
  • Grassy or muddy: shorten the green infusion and lower extraction temperature.

Molecule Lens

The cues that make it read as absinthe

  • trans-Anethole: anise-fennel core and the category’s primary signature.
  • Fenchone: fennel’s cooler, greener counterpoint to anethole.
  • Linalool: coriander’s floral-citrus bridge.
  • Citral: lemon balm’s bright herbal edge.
  • Pinene family: angelica, hyssop, and green herbs pulling the blend alpine rather than candy-sweet.
